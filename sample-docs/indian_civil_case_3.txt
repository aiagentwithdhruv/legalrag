I. BACKGROUND

1. This miscellaneous petition has been filed under Article 227 of the
Constitution of India seeking to challenge the order dated 15.11.2023 passed
by the Additional District Judge, Family Court, Indore, in Matrimonial Case
No. 214 of 2021, whereby the learned Family Court rejected the petitioner's
application under Section 24 of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, seeking interim
maintenance pendente lite and litigation expenses.

2. The factual matrix is as follows: The petitioner, Smt. Suveechi Chaudhary,
and the respondent No. 1, Dr. Ambuj Chaudhary, were married on 26th November
1992 according to Hindu rites and ceremonies at Indore. The couple has one
daughter, Ms. Priyanka Chaudhary, born on 8th October 1993, who is now an
adult and is employed.

3. The matrimonial relationship between the parties deteriorated significantly
over the years. Respondent No. 1 filed a petition for divorce under Section
13(1)(i-a) and Section 13(1)(i) of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, alleging
cruelty and adultery respectively against the petitioner (Matrimonial Case
No. 214 of 2021 before the Family Court, Indore).

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

II. FACTS IN DETAIL

4. The parties lived together as husband and wife from 1992 until approximately
2018, a period of over 25 years. During this period, the petitioner was a
homemaker who managed the household and raised their daughter, while the
respondent pursued his career as a cardiac surgeon, rising to the position of
Director of Cardiac Surgery at Fortis Hospital, Phase 8, Mohali, Punjab.

5. The respondent's income, as disclosed in his income tax returns for
Assessment Year 2023-24, is Rs. 9,00,000/- (Rupees Nine Lakhs) per month.
He additionally receives perquisites, allowances, accommodation, and various
other benefits from Fortis Hospital, which the petitioner estimates brings
his effective monthly income to approximately Rs. 12-14 lakhs.

6. The petitioner submits she has no independent source of income. She is 55
years of age and has been a homemaker throughout the marriage. She has some
investments in shares worth approximately Rs. 76,00,000/- (Rupees Seventy-Six
Lakhs) which were gifted to her by her parents at the time of marriage and
through occasional gifts thereafter. She resides at a family property in Dhar,
Madhya Pradesh.

7. The petitioner filed an application under Section 24 of the Hindu Marriage
Act, 1955, seeking:
   (a) Interim maintenance of Rs. 5,00,000/- per month;
   (b) Litigation expenses of Rs. 10,00,000/- (lump sum).

8. The Family Court, by its order dated 15.11.2023, rejected the Section 24
application, recording the following findings:
   - The petitioner has been filing income tax returns showing income;
   - The petitioner owns shares worth Rs. 76 lakhs;
   - The petitioner resides at a "palace property" in Dhar;
   - Therefore, the petitioner is "self-sufficient" and not entitled to
     maintenance.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

III. ISSUES FOR DETERMINATION

9. The following questions arise for this Court's determination:

(i)   Whether the Family Court's rejection of the Section 24 application is
      legally sustainable given the significant income disparity between the
      parties?

(ii)  Whether a spouse's possession of some personal assets or investments
      disentitles her from claiming interim maintenance under the Hindu Marriage
      Act?

(iii) What standard should be applied in determining the quantum of interim
      maintenance under Section 24?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IV. SUBMISSIONS

PETITIONER'S SUBMISSIONS:

10. Mr. Aditya Shrivastava, learned Senior Counsel for the petitioner, submitted
as follows:

(a) The Family Court committed a manifest error in equating the possession of
inherited assets (shares worth Rs. 76 lakhs) with the capacity to maintain
oneself at the standard of living enjoyed in the matrimonial home.

(b) The respondent's monthly income is Rs. 9,00,000/- — this is a matter of
record from his own tax filings. The lifestyle maintained in the matrimonial
home included domestic staff, a car, private schooling for the daughter,
premium medical facilities, and social obligations commensurate with a senior
doctor's status.

(c) The petitioner cannot be expected to liquidate her parental assets simply
because she needs funds during litigation. These assets are a security for
her future, not a substitute for maintenance during pending proceedings.

(d) The Supreme Court has categorically held in Rajnesh v. Neha (2021) 2 SCC
324 that "the purpose of Section 24 is to ensure that the less financially
able spouse is not disadvantaged during litigation." The provision is designed
to provide litigation support, not merely survival money.

(e) The petitioner, at 55 years of age, with no professional qualifications
or recent employment history, has zero earning capacity in the formal economy.
The shares are illiquid — she cannot sell a portion of shares every month to
meet household and legal expenses.

(f) The litigation is complex, involving serious allegations of adultery and
cruelty. She requires funds for engaging experienced legal counsel, travel,
court appearances, and other associated expenses.

RESPONDENT'S SUBMISSIONS:

11. Mr. Puneet Bhatt, learned counsel for Respondent No. 1, submitted:

(a) The petitioner is not without means. She has been filing income tax returns,
indicating sources of income. Her shares portfolio worth Rs. 76 lakhs generates
dividend income. The "palace property" in Dhar is a substantial asset.

(b) The petitioner's ITR shows a reported income, and she is therefore not
entirely dependent on the respondent.

(c) The standard of Section 24 is not that the spouse seeking maintenance must
be penniless — it is that she lacks sufficient independent income to support
herself and meet litigation expenses. Given the petitioner's assets, she does
have sufficient resources.

(d) The petition for divorce was compelled by the respondent's suffering due
to the petitioner's conduct. He is not obligated to maintain a spouse who
has inflicted cruelty and committed adultery.

(e) The amount of Rs. 5 lakhs per month sought is grossly excessive and
disproportionate, amounting to more than 55% of the respondent's monthly income.

V. ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS

A. LEGAL FRAMEWORK - SECTION 24, HINDU MARRIAGE ACT, 1955:

12. Section 24 of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, provides:

"Where in any proceeding under this Act it appears to the court that either
the wife or the husband, as the case may be, has no independent income
sufficient for her or his support and the necessary expenses of the proceeding,
it may, on the application of the wife or the husband, order the respondent
to pay to the petitioner the expenses of the proceeding, and monthly during
the proceeding such sum as, having regard to the petitioner's own income and
the income of the respondent, it may seem to the court to be reasonable."

B. EARNING CAPACITY VERSUS ACTUAL INCOME:

13. This Court rejects the Family Court's reasoning. The Supreme Court in
Shailja v. Khobbanna (2018) 12 SCC 199 has unequivocally held that "merely
because a wife is capable of earning would not be a sufficient reason to
reduce the maintenance." The principle extends with greater force when the wife
is not merely capable but actually earning less than is necessary to maintain
the standard of living of the matrimonial home.

14. The petitioner's shares portfolio generates some dividend income, but this
is irregular and cannot substitute for the regular monthly income necessary
for day-to-day living and litigation expenses. The Family Court conflated the
possession of capital assets with having a "sufficient independent income."
Capital assets and monthly income are distinct concepts in law.

C. STANDARD OF LIVING IN MATRIMONIAL HOME:

15. The Supreme Court in Rajnesh v. Neha (2021) 2 SCC 324 established a
comprehensive framework for determining maintenance, directing courts to
consider: (i) the standard of living of the parties during marriage; (ii)
the income and earning capacity of both parties; (iii) the reasonable needs
of the claimant; and (iv) any existing financial arrangements.

16. The respondent's income of Rs. 9 lakhs per month is established from his
own tax filings. The lifestyle commensurate with such income — in a metro city,
as a senior hospital director — inevitably involves expenditure on housing,
transport, social obligations, healthcare, and other necessities at a premium
level. The petitioner, accustomed to this standard for 25 years, cannot be
expected to drastically reduce her living standard during pending litigation.

D. QUANTUM OF MAINTENANCE:

17. Having found that the petitioner is entitled to interim maintenance, this
Court must determine the appropriate quantum. The following factors are
relevant:
   - Respondent's net monthly income: Rs. 9,00,000/-
   - Petitioner's age: 55 years
   - Duration of marriage: 25+ years
   - Petitioner's limited earning capacity
   - Complexity of litigation

18. This Court finds that maintenance of Rs. 1,00,000/- per month is fair
and reasonable. This represents approximately 11% of the respondent's monthly
income, which is neither punitive nor inadequate for the petitioner's
legitimate needs during the pendency of proceedings.

VI. ORDER

19. For the reasons stated above, this petition is ALLOWED.

20. The impugned order dated 15.11.2023 passed by the Additional District
Judge, Family Court, Indore, in Matrimonial Case No. 214 of 2021, is QUASHED
AND SET ASIDE.

21. Respondent No. 1, Dr. Ambuj Chaudhary, is directed to pay:

(a) INTERIM MAINTENANCE: Rs. 1,00,000/- (Rupees One Lakh) per month to the
petitioner from the date of her application before the Family Court (i.e.,
from the date the Section 24 application was first filed), pending the final
disposal of Matrimonial Case No. 214 of 2021.

(b) LITIGATION EXPENSES: Rs. 50,000/- (Rupees Fifty Thousand) as a lump sum
toward litigation expenses, payable within 30 days.

22. Arrears of interim maintenance (from the date of the Section 24 application
to date) shall be deposited before the Family Court within 60 days. Thereafter,
monthly maintenance shall be paid by the 7th of each calendar month.

23. The Family Court is directed to expeditiously dispose of Matrimonial Case
No. 214 of 2021, preferably within 18 months from the date of this order.

24. This order shall not be construed as an expression of opinion on the merits
of the divorce petition, which shall be decided independently by the Family Court.

25. No order as to costs.
