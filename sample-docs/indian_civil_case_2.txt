I. BACKGROUND

1. The present complaint has been filed under Section 17 of the Consumer
Protection Act, 1986 (as applicable at the time of filing) by the complainant,
Mr. Vijayendra Deogan, against Jai Prakash Associates Ltd. (hereinafter referred
to as "the builder" or "Jaypee Greens"), a large real estate developer
operating the Jaypee Greens integrated township project in Sector 128, Noida,
Uttar Pradesh.

2. The complaint primarily seeks: (a) delivery of possession of the booked
residential apartment; (b) compensation for deficiency in service; (c) damages
for mental agony, harassment, and financial loss; and (d) reimbursement of
rental expenses incurred due to the builder's failure to deliver possession.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

II. FACTS OF THE CASE

3. The builder launched the Jaypee Greens integrated township project in 2008
with extensive advertising campaigns promising world-class amenities and timely
delivery of residential units. The complainant's parents, being impressed by
the representations made in the builder's advertising and sales presentations,
applied for and were allotted residential apartment Unit No. P5-806 in the
project.

4. The following payments were made by the complainant's family towards the
allotment:

   Date of Payment      Amount (Rs.)     Cumulative Total
   ─────────────────    ─────────────    ────────────────
   July 2008            8,00,000         8,00,000
   August 2008          5,00,000         13,00,000
   September 2008       4,34,150         17,34,150
   November 2008        6,00,000         23,34,150

   Total Amount Deposited: Rs. 23,34,150/- (Rupees Twenty-Three Lakhs
   Thirty-Four Thousand One Hundred and Fifty only)

5. The provisional allotment letter dated 21st July 2008 explicitly stated:
"The possession of the said apartment is expected to be delivered to you within
a period of 36 (Thirty-Six) months from the date of this letter." This meant
the contractual possession date was approximately July 2011.

6. In April 2012, the unit was transferred to the complainant (son), and an
amended allotment letter was issued. Furthermore, the allotted unit was changed
from P5-806 to K12-1203 in Kalypso Court, with a revised area of 1,885 square
feet. The complainant accepted this change under the assurance that it was an
equivalent or superior unit.

7. As of December 2012 — eighteen months past the promised delivery date — the
builder had still not delivered possession, and construction appeared grossly
incomplete. In March 2013, the builder sent a demand letter claiming Rs.
42,79,678/- was due from the complainant, with a payment deadline of thirty
days, failing which the allotment would stand cancelled.

8. The complainant, shocked by this demand on an incomplete unit, inspected
the project site and found construction stalled. The complainant was residing
in rental accommodation in New Delhi at a monthly rental of Rs. 35,000/-.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

III. ISSUES FRAMED

9. The following issues arise for determination:

(i)   Whether the builder committed deficiency in service by failing to deliver
      possession within the promised 36-month period?

(ii)  Whether the complainant is entitled to compensation for the delay and the
      consequent financial and mental hardship?

(iii) What is the appropriate rate of interest on deposited amounts?

(iv)  Whether the builder is liable for rental expenses incurred by the
      complainant due to the delay?

(v)   Whether the threatened allotment cancellation is valid and enforceable?

(vi)  What is the total quantum of damages, compensation, and costs payable?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IV. ARGUMENTS OF THE PARTIES

COMPLAINANT'S ARGUMENTS:

10. Mr. Rohit Sharma, learned counsel for the complainant, advanced the following:

(a) The provisional allotment letter of July 21, 2008, contains an express and
binding promise to deliver possession within 36 months, i.e., by July 2011.
This is a contractual obligation, the breach of which amounts to deficiency
in service under Section 2(1)(g) of the Consumer Protection Act, 1986.

(b) The complainant made substantial payments totaling Rs. 23,34,150/- in good
faith, based on the builder's representations and promises. It is unconscionable
for the builder to now demand further payments on an incomplete project without
fulfilling its primary obligation of construction and delivery.

(c) The complainant has been deprived of a home he paid for and has been forced
to live in rented accommodation at considerable personal expense for over twelve
years (from July 2011 till date of judgment). The financial loss, mental agony,
and disruption to his family life cannot be adequately compensated by mere
return of the deposited amount.

(d) The builder's threat of allotment cancellation is unlawful. A party in
default of its primary contractual obligation cannot seek to penalize the other
party for non-payment of further installments on an incomplete project. A
defaulting party cannot forfeit the deposits of the innocent party.

(e) The builder has failed to obtain mandatory legal certifications including
the Completion Certificate and Occupancy Certificate, indicating the building
is not legally fit for habitation even if physically complete.

BUILDER'S ARGUMENTS:

11. Mr. Sanjay Kumar Gupta, learned counsel for Jai Prakash Associates Ltd.,
submitted:

(a) The agreement between the parties is governed by a construction-linked
payment plan. The complainant failed to adhere to the agreed payment schedule
and has an outstanding due of Rs. 62,55,593/- as of September 2014.

(b) The builder cannot be expected to complete construction and deliver
possession when the purchaser has not fulfilled his financial obligations under
the agreement. The complainant's default in payment is the primary cause of
the delay.

(c) No specific 36-month delivery guarantee was given. The allotment letter
uses the word "expected," which is aspirational and not a binding contractual
commitment. Real estate development is subject to multiple variables including
regulatory approvals, market conditions, and supply chain factors.

(d) The cancellation notice was issued legitimately and in accordance with
the terms of the allotment agreement, which provides for cancellation upon
default in payment. The complainant cannot resist cancellation while owing
substantial sums to the builder.

(e) The complaint ought to be dismissed as the complainant has not complied with
his contractual obligations.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

V. ANALYSIS

12. The Commission has carefully considered the submissions of both parties,
examined the documents on record, and applied the relevant law.

A. EXPRESS PROMISE OF 36-MONTH DELIVERY:

13. The provisional allotment letter dated July 21, 2008, is reproduced here
in the relevant part: "the possession of the said apartment is expected to be
delivered to you within a period of 36 (Thirty Six) months." The Commission
rejects the builder's contention that the word "expected" renders this
provision non-binding. In the context of a commercial real estate transaction
where a consumer parts with substantial consideration, such representations
constitute contractual warranties. This position is supported by the Supreme
Court's holding in Pioneer Urban Land & Infrastructure Ltd. v. Govindan
Raghavan (2019) 5 SCC 725.

B. CONSTRUCTION-LINKED PAYMENT AND DEFAULT:

14. The Commission notes that the complainant has deposited Rs. 23,34,150/-
and disputes the further demands. The builder, who has been unable to show
any meaningful construction progress despite thirteen years, cannot plausibly
claim the complainant's alleged payment default caused the delay. The builder
bears the primary obligation of completing construction; a purchaser's
withholding of installments after significant delay is self-protective conduct,
not contractual default.

C. MANDATORY CERTIFICATES NOT OBTAINED:

15. The builder was unable to produce:
    (a) Completion Certificate from the competent authority;
    (b) Occupancy Certificate as required under applicable building regulations;
    (c) No Objection Certificates from the Pollution Control Board, Fire
        Department, and Aviation Authority;
    (d) Any construction progress documentation or status reports.

This comprehensive failure demonstrates not merely delay but potentially
fundamental non-compliance with legal requirements for habitable residential
construction.

D. CANCELLATION NOT VALID:

16. The law is well settled that a party who is itself in breach of the
primary obligation under a contract cannot lawfully invoke a forfeiture clause
against the other party. In Ireo Grace Realtech Pvt. Ltd. v. Abhishek Khanna
(2021) 3 SCC 241, the Supreme Court held that a builder who fails to deliver
possession cannot forfeit the buyer's deposit. The cancellation notice is
accordingly null and void.

E. COMPENSATION QUANTUM:

17. The Commission, applying the principles laid down by the Supreme Court and
considering the facts that:
   - The delay has persisted since July 2011 (twelve years plus);
   - The complainant has paid Rs. 23,34,150/- without receiving possession;
   - The complainant has borne rental expenses of Rs. 35,000/- per month;
   - Significant mental agony and harassment have been suffered;

awards the following relief:

VI. FINAL ORDER / JUDGMENT

18. The State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, Uttar Pradesh, Lucknow,
hereby ALLOWS this complaint and directs as follows:

ORDER 1 - POSSESSION:
The Opposite Parties are directed to deliver possession of Flat No. K-12, Block
Kalypso Court, Jaypee Greens, Sector 128, Noida, admeasuring 1,885 square feet,
complete in all respects, with Completion Certificate and Occupancy Certificate,
and to execute the registered sale deed within 30 days of this order.

In the event of failure to deliver possession and execute the sale deed within
30 days, the Opposite Parties shall pay to the complainant a sum of Rs.
1,00,000/- (Rupees One Lakh) per month from the expiry of the 30-day period
until the date of actual possession and registration.

ORDER 2 - INTEREST ON DEPOSITED AMOUNT:
The Opposite Parties shall pay interest at the rate of 12% per annum on the
deposited amount of Rs. 23,34,150/- from the respective dates of each deposit
until the date of actual possession, if possession is delivered within 30 days.
If possession is not delivered within 30 days, interest shall be payable at
15% per annum from respective dates of deposit.

ORDER 3 - COMPENSATION FOR DEFICIENCY IN SERVICE:
The Opposite Parties shall pay Rs. 5,00,000/- (Rupees Five Lakhs) as
compensation for deficiency in service, with interest at 12% per annum from
01.07.2011 if paid within 30 days, and at 15% per annum if not paid within
30 days.

ORDER 4 - RENTAL COMPENSATION:
The Opposite Parties shall pay Rs. 15,000/- per month from 01.07.2011 (the
date when possession ought to have been delivered) until the date of actual
possession, as compensation towards rental expenses, without interest if paid
within 30 days; at 12% per annum thereafter.

ORDER 5 - CANCELLATION SET ASIDE:
Any notice of cancellation of allotment issued by the Opposite Parties is
hereby declared null and void. No interest shall be charged on the balance
amount of the apartment cost. The total confirmed cost of the apartment is
fixed at Rs. 60,82,000/-.

ORDER 6 - MENTAL AGONY:
The Opposite Parties shall pay Rs. 30,00,000/- (Rupees Thirty Lakhs) as
compensation for mental agony, harassment, and suffering, with interest at
12% per annum from 01.07.2011 if paid within 30 days; at 15% per annum
thereafter.

ORDER 7 - COSTS:
The Opposite Parties shall pay Rs. 50,000/- toward litigation costs.

19. All decreetal amounts are payable within 30 days of this order, failing
which execution proceedings may be initiated by the complainant at the
Opposite Parties' expense.
