I. INTRODUCTION

1. This Civil Revision Petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India
has been filed by the 4th defendant in Original Suit No. 301 of 2023, pending
on the file of the Additional Sub Court, Madurai, assailing the trial court's
refusal to strike off the plaint in the said suit.

2. The suit (OS No. 301 of 2023) has been instituted by Respondent No. 1
(Santhanakrishnan, as Plaintiff) against the petitioner (Annadurai, as the 4th
Defendant) and other respondents, seeking partition of joint family property
and permanent injunction restraining the defendants from creating third-party
rights over the disputed properties.

3. The petitioner contends that the filing of OS No. 301 of 2023 is a clear
abuse of the process of court, as the subject matter of the suit was conclusively
adjudicated in a prior suit (OS No. 66 of 2012) which attained finality at
all three levels — the Trial Court, First Appellate Court, and the High Court —
all ruling against the plaintiff/respondent herein. The petitioner has moved
this Court seeking exercise of supervisory jurisdiction to directly strike off
the plaint as an abuse of process.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

II. BACKGROUND FACTS

4. The disputed properties consist of residential and commercial premises
situated at Door Nos. 4 and 7, Kannan Street, Rajaji Nagar, Madurai, along
with an adjacent vacant plot of land and rights in an oil mill (Guru Krishna
Oil Mill) operating on a portion of the suit property. The total estimated
market value of the disputed properties as of 2023 is approximately Rs. 2.5
crore.

5. The parties trace their rights through one Ramachandran, who was the
patriarch of the family and who died intestate in 2018. At the time of his
death, Ramachandran was survived by:
   - Santhanakrishnan (Respondent No. 1) — son
   - Senthilkumar (Respondent No. 2) — son
   - Viji (Respondent No. 3) — daughter
   - Amutha (Respondent No. 4) — daughter
   - Uma Maheswari (Respondent No. 5) — daughter
   - Annadurai (Petitioner) — nephew (son of Ramachandran's deceased brother)

6. The petitioner claims that by virtue of a registered settlement deed dated
15.03.1985, Ramachandran had during his lifetime transferred his share in the
joint family properties to the petitioner. Accordingly, when the properties
devolved, the petitioner contends he is entitled to a share as a settled
transferee, and the properties are not available for partition among
Ramachandran's children.

7. PRIOR LITIGATION HISTORY:

   OS No. 66 of 2012: The petitioner (Annadurai) filed this suit seeking
   declaration of his rights under the settlement deed of 1985 and permanent
   injunction against Ramachandran's children from interfering with his
   possession.

   Trial Court (2015): On 26.08.2015, the Trial Court passed a decree in
   favor of the petitioner, declaring his title and rights.

   First Appeal (2016): The respondents/defendants appealed. The First
   Appellate Court upheld the Trial Court's decree on 22.12.2016.

   Second Appeal (2017): The respondents filed a Second Appeal before the
   Madras High Court. The High Court dismissed the Second Appeal on 24.04.2017,
   affirming the decree in the petitioner's favor.

8. Despite having exhausted all available remedies in OS No. 66 of 2012 and
losing at all three stages, Respondent No. 1 (Santhanakrishnan) filed the
fresh suit OS No. 301 of 2023 with essentially the same subject matter —
seeking partition of the same properties and injunction against the same
defendant (petitioner herein).

9. The petitioner filed an application before the Trial Court seeking rejection
of the plaint under Order 7 Rule 11 of the Civil Procedure Code on the ground
of res judicata and abuse of process. The Trial Court dismissed this application.

10. The petitioner has now approached this Court under Article 227 seeking to
invoke the High Court's supervisory jurisdiction to directly strike off the
plaint.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

III. ISSUES FOR DETERMINATION

11. The following legal issues arise:

(i)   Whether this Court should, in exercise of supervisory jurisdiction under
      Article 227, directly strike off the plaint in OS No. 301 of 2023?

(ii)  Whether the filing of OS No. 301 of 2023, in circumstances where the
      same subject matter was finally decided in OS No. 66 of 2012 (affirmed
      up to the High Court), constitutes abuse of process?

(iii) Whether res judicata is a permissible ground for rejecting the plaint
      under Order 7 Rule 11 of the Code of Civil Procedure?

(iv)  What is the appropriate procedural remedy for the petitioner?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IV. SUBMISSIONS

PETITIONER'S SUBMISSIONS:

12. Mr. S. Krishnamurthy, learned Senior Counsel for the petitioner, argued
forcefully as follows:

(a) The filing of OS No. 301 of 2023 is a blatant abuse of the court's process.
Respondent No. 1 has already litigated the same property dispute through three
tiers of the judicial hierarchy — Trial Court, First Appellate Court, and High
Court — and lost at every stage. Filing a fresh suit on the same cause of action
against the same party for essentially the same relief constitutes a gross abuse
of process that this Court should not countenance.

(b) The doctrine of res judicata, codified in Section 11 of the Code of Civil
Procedure, operates as an absolute bar. A matter once conclusively decided by
a court of competent jurisdiction cannot be re-litigated between the same
parties on the same cause of action. The 2023 suit is barred.

(c) The High Court's power under Article 227 is not merely appellate but
supervisory. Where a proceeding before a subordinate court is manifestly an
abuse of process, the High Court can intervene directly without requiring the
aggrieved party to exhaust alternative remedies.

(d) Requiring the petitioner to file an application under Order 7 Rule 11 before
the trial court — which has already rejected one such application — and then
potentially appeal through multiple tiers, imposes an unjust burden on the
petitioner who is fighting re-litigation of a settled matter.

RESPONDENTS' SUBMISSIONS:

13. Mr. T. Kumaresan, learned counsel for Respondents No. 1 to 5, countered:

(a) The Supreme Court has specifically held in Srihari Hanumandas Totala v.
Hemant Vithal Kamat (2021) 9 SCC 99 that a plaint cannot be rejected on the
ground of res judicata by invoking Order 7 Rule 11. Res judicata is a defense
which must be pleaded and proved at trial — it is not a ground on which the
plaint itself can be rejected at the threshold stage.

(b) For the purpose of an application under Order 7 Rule 11, only the plaint
averments are to be read. The defense of res judicata requires examination
of evidence from both sides, including the prior suit documents, decrees,
and findings — this cannot be done without a full trial.

(c) OS No. 301 of 2023 raises distinct reliefs and relies on events and
documents post-dating the earlier litigation. The suit is not a mere re-filing
of the same claim.

(d) The High Court's Article 227 jurisdiction must be exercised sparingly.
The Supreme Court has repeatedly cautioned against High Courts routinely
intervening in trial court proceedings. The appropriate remedy is to allow the
trial to proceed, with the petitioner raising res judicata as a defense.

V. ANALYSIS

A. SCOPE OF ARTICLE 227 JURISDICTION:

14. The supervisory jurisdiction of this Court under Article 227 of the
Constitution is well recognized. However, as the Supreme Court cautioned in
Virudhunagar Hindu Nadargal Dharma Paribalana Sabai v. Tuticorin Educational
Society (2019) 9 SCC 538: "The High Court in exercise of its supervisory
jurisdiction under Article 227 must be careful not to interfere with every
interlocutory order of subordinate courts. Such interference should be limited
to patent illegality, jurisdictional errors, or grave injustice."

15. The test is not whether this Court disagrees with the trial court's order,
but whether the trial court's order is so manifestly erroneous that it must
be corrected to prevent injustice.

B. RES JUDICATA AND ORDER 7 RULE 11:

16. The respondents have correctly invoked Srihari Hanumandas Totala v. Hemant
Vithal Kamat (2021) 9 SCC 99, where the Supreme Court comprehensively held
that res judicata cannot ground rejection of a plaint under Order 7 Rule 11
of the CPC. The Supreme Court's reasoning is compelling: Order 7 Rule 11
requires the court to read only the plaint allegations and determine, on the
face of the plaint, whether the suit is maintainable. Res judicata is a
defense that the defendant must establish through evidence.

17. This Court cannot depart from this binding Supreme Court precedent. The
Trial Court correctly refused to reject the plaint on the ground of res judicata
at the Order 7 Rule 11 stage.

C. ABUSE OF PROCESS — A DISTINCT GROUND:

18. However, the petitioner has also raised the ground of "suppression of
material facts" and "abuse of the process of court" which is conceptually
distinct from res judicata. While res judicata requires factual examination,
a blatant and demonstrable abuse of process — such as filing a suit while
suppressing the existence and outcome of a prior identical suit — may, in
appropriate cases, empower the court to take action.

19. However, this Court holds that even for abuse of process, the appropriate
first forum is the trial court itself, through an appropriate application.
The petitioner did file an Order 7 Rule 11 application, which was dismissed.
If the grounds for the application included suppression of prior litigation,
the petitioner can file a separate application specifically raising the abuse
of process ground, distinct from res judicata.

20. Alternatively, if the plaint does not disclose the existence of the prior
suit (OS No. 66 of 2012) and its outcome, the petitioner's defense in the
written statement — which will draw the trial court's attention to this
suppression — will be the appropriate vehicle for raising this issue.

D. APPROPRIATE REMEDY:

21. This Court finds that invoking Article 227 at this stage to directly strike
off the plaint would be premature and would bypass the trial court's proper
role. The trial court has a full array of procedural remedies — including the
power to examine whether the suit is barred by res judicata as a preliminary
issue, the power to frame issues, and the power to dismiss the suit summarily
if the facts are undisputed.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

VI. ORDER

22. The Civil Revision Petition is DISMISSED for the following reasons:

(i)  Res judicata is not a ground for rejection of plaint under Order 7 Rule 11
     CPC (Srihari Hanumandas Totala). The Trial Court correctly dismissed
     the Order 7 Rule 11 application.

(ii) This Court's supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227 should not be
     invoked at this preliminary stage to bypass the trial court's processes.

23. However, this Court grants the following liberty and directions:

(i)  The petitioner is granted liberty to file an appropriate application before
     the Trial Court raising the ground of suppression of prior litigation and
     abuse of process, as a ground distinct from res judicata. The trial court
     shall decide such application in accordance with law and relevant precedent.

(ii) The Trial Court may, at its discretion, frame res judicata as a preliminary
     issue under Order 14 Rule 2 CPC and decide it before proceeding with the
     full trial, if the relevant documents are not in dispute.

(iii) The Trial Court is directed to decide OS No. 301 of 2023 expeditiously,
      given the prolonged nature of the parties' disputes and the fact that the
      petitioner has been litigating this property matter since 2012.

24. The connected Miscellaneous Petition is closed. No costs.

1. Article 227 of the Constitution of India — Supervisory Jurisdiction of
   High Courts over all Courts and Tribunals in their territory

2. Section 11, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 — Res Judicata:
   "No Court shall try any suit or issue in which the matter directly and
   substantially in issue has been directly and substantially in issue in
   a former suit between the same parties... in a Court competent to try
   such subsequent suit..."

3. Order 7 Rule 11, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 — Rejection of Plaint:
   The plaint shall be rejected in the following cases:
   (a) where it does not disclose a cause of action;
   (b) where the relief claimed is undervalued;
   (c) where it appears from the statement in the plaint that the suit is
       barred by any law...

   judgment on all issues — Power to decide a preliminary issue first.
