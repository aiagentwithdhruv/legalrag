I. BACKGROUND

1. This petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India has been filed
by the petitioner, Suresh S/O Bitthalrao Banmbal, seeking to quash and set
aside the order dated 01.03.2023 passed by the Sub-Divisional Officer (Revenue),
Pandurna, in Appeal No. 77/2023, which reversed the mutation order dated
12.08.1994 passed by the Tahsildar, Pandurna, in Mutation Case No. 143/1993-94.

2. The petitioner additionally prays that the original mutation order dated
12.08.1994 be restored and given effect.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

3. The dispute in the present case centers around agricultural land situated
in Survey No. 142/1, 142/2, 143, and 148 of Village Kalamgaon, Tahsil Pandurna,
District Chhindwara, Madhya Pradesh, admeasuring approximately 4.80 hectares
(hereinafter referred to as "the suit lands").

4. The petitioner claims title to the suit lands based on a registered Will
dated 03.07.1989 executed by one Smt. Kalabai W/O Late Sitaram Banmbal, who
was the petitioner's paternal aunt. The said Will allegedly bequeathed the
suit lands to the petitioner to the exclusion of the natural heirs (the
respondents No. 1 and 2, who are the deceased's collateral relatives).

5. Following the death of Smt. Kalabai in 1991, the petitioner applied for
mutation of the suit lands in the revenue records on the basis of the Will.
The Tahsildar, Pandurna, after examining the Will and hearing objections,
passed the mutation order on 12.08.1994 directing that the petitioner's name
be entered in the revenue records as the owner of the suit lands.

6. The respondents No. 1 and 2, being the natural heirs of the deceased and
disputing the Will's validity, challenged the mutation order by filing an
appeal. This appeal remained pending for several years and was eventually
decided by the Sub-Divisional Officer (Revenue) on 01.03.2023, who reversed
the Tahsildar's mutation order.

7. The Sub-Divisional Officer's reasoning was that revenue authorities lack
jurisdiction to effect mutation based on a Will, since adjudication of a Will's
validity falls within the exclusive domain of civil courts. The appeal was
thus allowed and the mutation was set aside.

8. Aggrieved by this order, the petitioner has approached this Court.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

III. ISSUES FOR DETERMINATION

9. The following issues arise:

(i)   Do revenue authorities have jurisdiction to effect mutation of agricultural
      land based on a Will?

(ii)  What is the legal effect of a mutation entry in revenue records — does it
      create, extinguish, or confer title?

(iii) Whether the Sub-Divisional Officer was correct in setting aside the
      Tahsildar's mutation on the ground of lack of jurisdiction?

(iv)  What reliefs, if any, is the petitioner entitled to?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IV. SUBMISSIONS

PETITIONER'S SUBMISSIONS:

10. Mr. Deepak Joshi, learned counsel for the petitioner, submitted:

(a) The Tahsildar's mutation order of 1994 was passed after due inquiry and
after hearing all the parties. The respondents had the opportunity to object to
the mutation and participated in the mutation proceedings. The order attained
finality and has been in existence for thirty years.

(b) The petitioner has been in possession of the suit lands since 1991 and
has been cultivating the agricultural land. Reversal of the mutation after
thirty years causes serious prejudice to the petitioner who has invested in
the land over decades.

(c) While the petitioner concedes (as required by Supreme Court precedents)
that revenue authorities cannot adjudicate the validity of a Will as such,
he submits that the mutation order should be restored in a conditional manner,
subject to the outcome of the pending civil suit for title declaration.

(d) The petitioner has already filed a civil suit (Civil Suit No. 88/2023)
before the Civil Judge, Senior Division, Chhindwara, for declaration of title
based on the Will of Smt. Kalabai. The mutation, if restored conditionally,
would not prejudice the respondents since the civil suit will determine the
ultimate question of title.

RESPONDENTS' SUBMISSIONS:

11. Mr. Anil Sharma, learned counsel for Respondents No. 1 and 2, submitted:

(a) The Sub-Divisional Officer correctly held that revenue authorities cannot
mutate property records on the basis of a Will. The validity of the Will of
Smt. Kalabai is genuinely disputed — there are allegations that the Will was
forged or obtained under undue influence when the deceased was in ill health.

(b) The respondents are the natural legal heirs of the deceased and are entitled
to inherit the suit lands under the Hindu Succession Act, 1956, in the absence
of a valid Will.

(c) The mutation entry in revenue records, if restored, would create a false
impression of title and prejudice the respondents' case in the civil suit.
Revenue entries, though not conclusive of title, can create practical
difficulties and may be misused.

(d) The proper remedy for the petitioner is to establish his rights through
the civil court in Civil Suit No. 88/2023. Until the civil court determines
the validity of the Will, the revenue records should reflect the natural
succession position.

GOVERNMENT PLEADER'S SUBMISSIONS:

12. Mr. Pankaj Dubey, learned Government Pleader, submitted that the State
Government takes a neutral position on the merits of the title dispute. The
revenue administration's concern is that mutation records reflect the legally
correct position as determined by courts of competent jurisdiction.

V. ANALYSIS OF LAW

A. JURISDICTIONAL COMPETENCE OF REVENUE AUTHORITIES:

13. The law on this point is settled by a series of Supreme Court decisions.
The question whether revenue authorities can mutate land records on the basis
of a Will was directly addressed in Balwant Singh v. Daulat Singh (1997) 7 SCC
137, where the Supreme Court held: "Revenue authorities have no jurisdiction
to adjudicate upon the genuineness or validity of a Will. Such determination
falls within the exclusive domain of civil courts."

14. This position was reiterated in Thakur Sukhpal Singh v. Thakur Kalyan
Singh AIR 1963 SC 146 and followed consistently by this Court. The learned
counsel for the petitioner, when confronted with this settled position, fairly
conceded that the Sub-Divisional Officer was correct in the legal principle.

B. NATURE AND EFFECT OF MUTATION ENTRIES:

15. The second crucial legal principle is equally well established. In Suraj
Bhan v. Financial Commissioner (2007) 6 SCC 186, the Supreme Court categorically
held: "Mutation of property in revenue records neither creates nor extinguishes
title to the property. A mutation entry has no presumptive value on title. It
serves only a fiscal purpose — for collection of land revenue from whoever is
shown as the occupant."

16. This principle has been followed in numerous decisions of this Court and
the Supreme Court. The entry in revenue records cannot, by itself, prove
ownership. It is merely an administrative record maintained for fiscal purposes.

17. The significance of this principle to the present case is that even if the
Tahsildar's mutation order had been allowed to stand, it would not have
conferred title on the petitioner. Title can only be decided by a competent
civil court. Conversely, the reversal of the mutation entry does not extinguish
whatever rights the petitioner may have under the Will — those rights, if any,
can only be determined by the civil court.

C. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:

18. The petitioner has filed Civil Suit No. 88/2023 before the Civil Judge,
Senior Division, Chhindwara. This is the appropriate forum for determining the
validity of the Will and consequent title. The civil court alone can, after
examination of the original Will, questioning of witnesses, and full trial,
determine whether the Will of Smt. Kalabai was genuine, voluntary, and legally
valid.

19. Until the civil court determines the question of title, the status quo in
revenue records may be maintained as directed by the Sub-Divisional Officer.
This does not prejudice the petitioner's legal rights, which remain intact for
adjudication by the civil court.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

VI. ORDER

20. In view of the above analysis, this petition is DISMISSED. The order dated
01.03.2023 passed by the Sub-Divisional Officer (Revenue), Pandurna, is UPHELD
for the following reasons:

(i)  Revenue authorities have no jurisdiction to determine the validity of a
     Will. The Sub-Divisional Officer correctly set aside the Tahsildar's
     mutation which was premised on an assumption about the Will's validity.

(ii) The mutation entry in revenue records does not create or extinguish title.
     Therefore, the setting aside of the mutation does not deprive the petitioner
     of any rights he may legitimately hold under the Will.

21. This Court, however, directs as follows:

(i)  The Civil Judge, Senior Division, Chhindwara, hearing Civil Suit No.
     88/2023, shall decide the suit strictly on its merits, without being
     influenced by the revenue authority's findings in either the Tahsildar's
     mutation order or the Sub-Divisional Officer's reversal order. Both orders
     are irrelevant to the question of title.

(ii) The civil court shall endeavor to decide Civil Suit No. 88/2023 within
     two years from the date of this order.

(iii) Pending the civil court's decision, both parties shall maintain status
      quo as regards physical possession and cultivation of the suit lands.

22. It is made clear that this Court has not expressed any opinion on the
validity or genuineness of the Will of Smt. Kalabai dated 03.07.1989, which
shall be determined exclusively by the civil court.

23. No order as to costs.
