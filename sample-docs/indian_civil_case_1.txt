━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

I. INTRODUCTION

1. These appeals arise out of a judgment and order dated 24.08.2023 passed
by the High Court of Gujarat at Ahmedabad in Civil Application (OJ) No.
176 of 2021, whereby the learned Single Judge of the High Court allowed the
said application filed by the respondent under Section 11(6) of the
Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (hereinafter referred to as "the
Act, 1996") and appointed Justice K.A. Puj (Retd.), Former Judge of the
Gujarat High Court, as sole arbitrator to adjudicate the disputes between
the parties.

2. The central question of law that arises for consideration in these
appeals is: Whether execution of a discharge voucher acknowledging "full and
final settlement" of insurance claims constitutes an absolute bar to the
invocation of arbitration, particularly when the signatory later disputes the
voluntary nature of such execution on grounds of coercion and financial
distress?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

II. BACKGROUND AND FACTS

3. The respondent, M/s Krish Spinning, is a partnership firm engaged in
the business of cotton spinning. The appellant, SBI General Insurance Co.
Ltd., is a general insurance company duly registered under the Insurance Act,
1938.

4. On 31.03.2018, the respondent obtained a Fire and Special Perils Policy
from the appellant bearing Policy No. 7101000119P102267419, providing coverage
of Rs. 7,20,00,000/- (Rupees Seven Crore Twenty Lakhs only) for the policy
period 31.03.2018 to 30.03.2019.

FIRST FIRE INCIDENT - 28.05.2018:

5. During the subsistence of the policy, the respondent's factory premises
suffered a fire incident on 28.05.2018. The respondent submitted a claim of
Rs. 1,76,19,967/- (Rupees One Crore Seventy-Six Lakhs Nineteen Thousand Nine
Hundred and Sixty-Seven only). The appellant deputed a licensed independent
surveyor and loss assessor to assess the actual loss suffered.

6. After thorough survey, the surveyor assessed the loss at Rs. 84,19,579/-
(Rupees Eighty-Four Lakhs Nineteen Thousand Five Hundred and Seventy-Nine
only). The respondent, through its authorized partner Mr. Narendrabhai
Paragbhai Dhamsaniya, executed a consent letter dated 24.12.2018 specifically
accepting the surveyor's assessed amount as full and final settlement. On
04.01.2019, the respondent executed an advance discharge voucher confirming
the receipt of Rs. 84,08,957/- as "full and final settlement of all claims,
demands, rights, and causes of action" arising from the first fire. The
appellant accordingly released this amount on 31.01.2019.

SECOND FIRE INCIDENT - 17.11.2018:

7. Before the settlement of the first claim, a second fire incident occurred
at the respondent's factory premises on 17.11.2018. This claim was much larger,
valued at approximately Rs. 8 crore. The appellant processed this claim and
released the full settled amount of Rs. 4,86,67,050/- in installments,
with the final payment made on 14.10.2019.

EMERGENCE OF THE DISPUTE:

8. Barely eleven days after receiving the final payment for the second claim
(i.e., on 25.10.2019), the respondent addressed a letter to the appellant
making startling allegations: (a) that the surveyor's report for the first
claim was never shared with the respondent; and (b) that the discharge voucher
for the first claim was obtained by the appellant under duress and coercion,
taking advantage of the respondent's financially distressed situation owing
to the pending second claim.

9. The appellant rejected this letter vide its communication dated 06.11.2019
and subsequently provided the surveyor's report on 07.11.2019.

10. On 02.03.2020, the respondent issued a legal notice demanding payment
of the balance of its first claim (i.e., difference between Rs. 1,76,19,967/-
claimed and Rs. 84,08,957/- received = Rs. 92,11,010/-) and simultaneously
invoked the arbitration clause contained in the insurance policy. After
receiving no favorable response, the respondent filed a petition under Section
11(6) of the Act, 1996 on 25.10.2021 before the Gujarat High Court seeking
appointment of an arbitrator.

III. ISSUES FRAMED FOR DETERMINATION

11. The following questions of law arise for this Court's consideration:

(i)  Whether the execution of a full and final settlement discharge voucher
     constitutes an absolute bar to the invocation of arbitration?

(ii) What is the standard of judicial scrutiny applicable to referral courts
     under Section 11(6) of the Act, 1996 when a plea of "accord and
     satisfaction" is raised by one party?

(iii) Whether legislative amendments introducing Section 11(6-A) of the Act,
      1996 have altered the scope of power exercised by referral courts?

(iv) Whether the arbitration agreement embedded in an insurance policy survives
     the discharge of the underlying contract?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IV. SUBMISSIONS OF THE PARTIES

SUBMISSIONS ON BEHALF OF THE APPELLANT (SBI GENERAL INSURANCE):

12. Mr. Dushyant Dave, learned Senior Counsel appearing for the appellant,
submitted as follows:

(a) The respondent voluntarily and without any reservation signed the consent
letter dated 24.12.2018 and the discharge voucher dated 04.01.2019. The
signatory was an experienced businessman who understood the implications of
the documents executed.

(b) There is no evidence whatsoever of any "take it or leave it" offer or
any coercive conduct by the appellant. The allegations of coercion were
raised fourteen months after the payment of the first claim was received and
eleven days after full payment of the second claim.

(c) The inordinate delay in raising the coercion plea demolishes the
respondent's case. A genuine victim of coercion would have raised objections
contemporaneously, not after profiting from a second insurance settlement.

(d) Accord and satisfaction extinguishes the underlying contractual rights.
When a contract is discharged by performance or settlement, the arbitration
clause embedded therein also stands discharged, as there is no longer any
dispute arising "under" or "in relation to" the contract.

(e) The claim is stale and the respondent's conduct amounts to forum shopping
and abuse of process.

SUBMISSIONS ON BEHALF OF THE RESPONDENT (M/S KRISH SPINNING):

13. Mr. Abhishek Manu Singhvi, learned Senior Counsel for the respondent,
countered:

(a) The respondent was in acute financial distress at the time of signing the
discharge voucher. The pending claim of approximately Rs. 8 crore for the
second fire created an impossible situation—the respondent needed to keep the
insurer satisfied to recover the larger amount and was therefore compelled to
accept the reduced settlement for the first claim.

(b) The letter dated 25.10.2019 was sent within eleven days of the final
settlement of the second claim, demonstrating the respondent's promptness
once the coercive pressure was removed.

(c) The arbitration agreement is a separate and autonomous agreement from the
underlying contract. Even if the main contract is discharged, the arbitration
clause survives under the doctrine of separability enshrined in Section 16(1)
of the Act, 1996.

(d) Whether the discharge voucher was signed under coercion is itself a
disputed question of fact that must be decided by the arbitrator, not by the
referral court. The Section 11(6) jurisdiction is limited to examining whether
a valid arbitration agreement exists.

(e) The post-2015 legislative amendments mandatorily restrict the Section 11(6)
court to examining only the existence of the arbitration agreement.

V. COURT'S ANALYSIS AND REASONING

A. PRELIMINARY OBJECTIONS:

14. The Court rejected two preliminary objections raised by the appellant:

(i)  No admission of liability: The Court found that liability was never
     disputed—both claims were processed and paid by the insurer. The dispute
     concerned only the quantum, i.e., whether the first claim settlement
     was inadequate.

(ii) Limitation: The respondent raised the coercion plea in October 2019, filed
     legal notice in March 2020, and the Section 11(6) petition in October 2021.
     This was within the applicable three-year limitation period from the date
     the cause of action arose.

B. ARBITRATION AGREEMENT SURVIVES DISCHARGE OF CONTRACT:

15. Drawing from the doctrine of separability under Section 16(1) of the Act,
1996, the Court firmly held that an arbitration clause does not perish merely
because the underlying substantive contract is discharged.

16. The Court reasoned: "An arbitration agreement is incorporated by the parties
with the mutual intention to settle all disputes arising under the substantive
contract, including disputes about the validity of the contract's termination
itself. The arbitration agreement is thus designed to outlast the contract it
accompanies, unless the parties expressly agree otherwise."

17. Where a party challenges the validity of a settlement (such as a discharge
voucher), that very challenge constitutes a dispute "arising in relation to"
the original contract, which falls squarely within the arbitration clause's
scope.

C. FULL AND FINAL SETTLEMENT - NOT AN ABSOLUTE BAR:

18. Relying upon the landmark Constitution Bench decision in National Insurance
Co. Ltd. v. Boghara Polyfab (P) Ltd. (2009) 1 SCC 267, the Court reiterated
that "mere execution of a discharge voucher is not an absolute bar to
arbitration when the validity of such execution is challenged on credible grounds
of fraud, coercion, or undue influence."

19. The Court distinguished between two categories of cases:
    - Category A: Where the discharge voucher is voluntarily executed with full
      knowledge after receipt of adequate consideration — arbitration is barred.
    - Category B: Where the discharge voucher's validity is challenged on grounds
      of coercion, fraud, or undue influence — the challenge itself constitutes
      an arbitrable dispute.

D. SCOPE OF SECTION 11(6) JURISDICTION POST-2015 AMENDMENTS:

20. The Court undertook extensive examination of the legislative history of
Section 11(6-A) introduced by the Arbitration and Conciliation (Amendment)
Act, 2015:

"Notwithstanding any judgment, decree or order of the Supreme Court or any
Court, the designation of an arbitral institution under sub-section (3A), the
designation of a person under sub-section (4) or the appointment of an
arbitrator under sub-sections (4) or (5) or (6), shall not be affected by the
judgment, decree or order of the Supreme Court or any Court regarding the
appointment of arbitrators, after such appointment is made by the arbitral
institution or person as the case may be."

21. The Court held that the 2015 amendment mandated that referral courts restrict
their examination strictly to the existence of the arbitration agreement. The
Court noted the evolution of judicial thinking:

Pre-2015 (Boghara Polyfab era): Courts could conduct limited examination of
accord and satisfaction defences.

Post-2015 (Mayavati Trading era): Legislative intent confines court to examining
whether a valid arbitration agreement exists — all other disputes go to the
arbitrator.

22. Citing Vidya Drolia v. Durga Trading Corporation (2021) 2 SCC 1, the Court
identified the narrow exceptions where referral courts may decline reference:
(a) When the arbitration agreement is manifestly non-existent or null and void;
(b) When the subject matter is demonstrably non-arbitrable;
(c) When the claim is ex facie frivolous, vexatious, or deadwood.

None of these exceptions applied to the present case.

E. APPLICATION TO PRESENT FACTS:

23. Evaluating the respondent's allegations, the Court found:

(a) The allegations of coercion were not bald or unsupported. The respondent
articulated a specific economic mechanism of coercion: a pending Rs. 8 crore
second claim created dependence on the insurer's goodwill, making resistance
to an under-settlement commercially impossible.

(b) The protest letter dated 25.10.2019 was sent eleven days after the final
settlement of the second claim—a timeframe consistent with someone freed from
coercive pressure, not with a party manufacturing a belated grievance.

(c) There was no prima facie evidence conclusively disproving the coercion
allegations at the Section 11(6) stage.

(d) Whether the discharge voucher was truly voluntary is a factual dispute best
resolved through evidence before an arbitrator, not through a mini-trial at
the referral stage.

VI. FINAL ORDER

24. For the foregoing reasons, this Court holds:

(i)  The arbitration clause in an insurance policy survives the execution of
     a discharge voucher for full and final settlement when the validity of
     such discharge voucher is itself under bona fide challenge.

(ii) Post-2015 amendments to the Act, 1996 restrict the referral court's
     jurisdiction under Section 11(6) to examining the existence of an
     arbitration agreement; disputed questions of coercion, fraud, or undue
     influence must be determined by the arbitrator.

(iii) The respondent's allegations of economic coercion are not ex facie
      frivolous and disclose a genuine triable dispute.

25. Both Civil Appeal Nos. 7821 and 7822 of 2024 are accordingly DISMISSED.

26. The appointment of Justice K.A. Puj (Retd.) as sole arbitrator by the
Gujarat High Court is UPHELD. The learned arbitrator shall proceed with the
arbitral proceedings in accordance with law.

27. The arbitrator is requested to expedite the proceedings given the
considerable time that has already elapsed since the first fire incident in
2018.

28. Costs shall be costs in the arbitration.
